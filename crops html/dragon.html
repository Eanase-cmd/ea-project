DRAGON FRUIT CULTIVATION METHOD

DRAGON FRUIT CULTIVATION METHOD

Dragon fruit, also known as pitaya, is a tropical fruit grown on a cactus species. It is gaining popularity due to its unique appearance, nutritional benefits, and market demand. Below is a detailed overview of the dragon fruit cultivation method, expected yield, income, and value-added products.

1. Land Preparation

• Soil pH: The soil pH should be between 6.0 to 7.0. • Ploughing: The field should be ploughed 2-3 times to break up the soil and create a fine seedbed. • Bed Preparation: Prepare raised beds or mounds to improve drainage, as dragon fruit plants are susceptible to waterlogging. • Fertilization: Apply organic matter like compost or well-decomposed farmyard manure (FYM) to the soil before planting to improve soil fertility. A balanced NPK fertilizer is recommended.

2. Propagation:

• Seed Rate:

• For Seed Propagation: About 1-2 grams of seeds is sufficient to plant 1 hectare. However, seeds can take a long time (1-2 years) to produce fruit. • For Cutting Propagation (Recommended): The best method is to use cuttings from mature, healthy dragon fruit plants. Typically, about 1,000-1,500 cuttings are needed for 1 hectare.

• Methods of Propagation:

Dragon fruit is generally propagated through cuttings rather than seeds because cuttings ensure faster fruiting (within 1-2 years compared to seeds, which take longer). • Cuttings: Select mature, healthy branches with at least 2-3 nodes for planting. Cuttings should be air-dried for 2-3 days before planting to prevent rot. • Seed Propagation (Not Recommended): Seeds can be used, but it takes about 4-5 years for plants to start fruiting.

3. Spacing and Planting:

• Spacing: o For high-density planting, maintain a spacing of 4m x 4m (625 trees per hectare). o For traditional spacing, the spacing is usually 6m x 6m (278 trees per hectare). • Planting Time: o The best time to plant guava is at the onset of the monsoon season (June-July) to ensure sufficient moisture for establishment. • Planting Method: o Dig a hole slightly larger than the root ball of the sapling. Ensure that the sapling is planted at the same depth as it was in the nursery. Water the plants immediately after planting to settle the soil around the roots.

4. Irrigation:

• Watering Needs: o Guava trees need regular watering, particularly during the dry season. Young trees need more frequent watering, while established trees need deeper watering. o Guava is drought-tolerant but grows better with consistent moisture. • Irrigation System: o Drip irrigation is the most efficient method for guava cultivation, delivering water directly to the root zone without causing waterlogging. • Water Management: o Ensure that the soil is not waterlogged, as this can harm the root system. Water only when the soil starts to dry out

5. Fertilization

• Initial Fertilization: o Apply well-decomposed compost or farmyard manure (FYM) at planting time. • Annual Fertilization: o Apply NPK fertilizers (10:10:10) at the beginning of the growing season. o Apply urea (nitrogenous fertilizer) during the vegetative phase for optimal growth. • Micronutrients: o Guava trees may require micronutrients like zinc and boron. Apply these nutrients as foliar sprays when deficiencies are observed.

6. Weeding and Pest Control:

• Weeding: o Regularly remove weeds manually or by using herbicides, especially during the early growth stages, to prevent competition for water and nutrients. • Pest Control: o Common pests affecting guava include aphids, mealybugs, and fruit flies. Neem oil, insecticides, or biological pest control methods can be used. • Diseases: o Guava trees are susceptible to fungal diseases like anthracnose and root rot. Use fungicides as needed and ensure proper drainage to avoid waterlogging, which can promote fungal growth.

7. Pruning:

• Pruning for Shape: o Regularly prune the trees to maintain their shape and remove dead or diseased branches. Proper pruning helps improve sunlight and air circulation, leading to better fruit quality. • Annual Pruning: o Prune guava trees after harvesting to control growth and maintain the health of the tree. Also, remove any unwanted growth or suckers.

8. Harvesting

• Harvesting Time: o Guava typically takes 8-12 months from flowering to fruit maturity, depending on the variety and climate conditions. The fruit is usually ready for harvest when it turns yellow-green or yellow (depending on the variety) and has a distinct aroma. • Harvesting Method: o Harvest the fruits by hand, gently twisting them off the tree. Use a sharp knife or pruning shear if necessary to avoid damaging the fruit or tree.

9. Post-Harvest Handling

•Cleaning: o After harvesting, wash the guavas to remove any dirt, chemicals, or debris. • Storage: o Guavas can be stored at room temperature for 1-2 weeks. For longer storage, refrigeration can be used, but the shelf life of guava is short (3-4 weeks). • Processing: o Guavas can be processed into guava juice, jam, jelly, or canned products to increase shelf life and marketability.

10. Expected Yield and Income

• Yield per Tree (Mature Trees): o A mature guava tree can produce 40-80 kg of fruit per year, depending on variety and growing conditions. • Yield per Acre (High-Density Planting): o With high-density planting (625 trees per hectare), the expected yield is around 25-40 tons per hectare per year. • Income per Acre: o If the market price is ₹20-50 per kg, then:  For 25 tons (25,000 kg): ₹5,00,000 to ₹12,50,000 per hectare  For 40 tons (40,000 kg): ₹8,00,000 to ₹20,00,000 per hectare

Expected income per hectare can range from ₹5,00,000 to ₹20,00,000, depending on yield and market prices.

11. Value-Added Products

1. Guava Juice 2. Guava Jam 3. Guava Jelly 4. Guava Pickle 5. Guava Syrup 6. Guava Powder 7. Guava Candy 8. Guava Pulp (for use in various processed foods) 9. Guava Tea 10. Canned Guava (for long-term storage and export)
