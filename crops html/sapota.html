SAPOTA (CHIKOO) CULTIVATION METHOD

SAPOTA (CHIKOO) CULTIVATION METHOD

Sapota (Chikoo) is a tropical fruit that thrives in warm, humid climates. It is a highly lucrative fruit crop grown for its delicious, sweet fruit. Below is a detailed overview of the cultivation method, expected yield, income, and value-added products.

1.Land Preparation

• Soil pH : soil pH should be between 6.0 to 7.5. • Ploughing: Plough the land 2-3 times to break the soil and to create a fine seedbed. The field should be well-drained to avoid waterlogging, as sapota is sensitive to excess moisture. • Pit Preparation: Prepare planting pits of size 45 cm x 45 cm x 45 cm (18” x 18” x 18”) for each sapling. Fill the pits with well-decomposed compost or farmyard manure (FYM), along with a small dose of phosphorus-based fertilizer. • Soil Fertilization: Apply well-decomposed organic matter (FYM or compost) in the planting pits. Additional fertilizers should be applied based on soil test recommendations.

2. Propagation:

• Seed rate: About 50-60 kg of seeds are required to plant 1 hectare if you are using seed propagation. • Methods of Propagation: Sapota is typically propagated by grafting, budding, or air layering. Grafting or budding ensures quicker fruiting, better yield, and uniformity in fruit quality. • Seed Propagation (Not Recommended): While sapota can be grown from seeds, trees from seeds take longer to bear fruit (6–7 years) and may not have desirable traits. For better results, use grafted or budded saplings. • Sapling Selection: Choose grafted or budded saplings for faster fruiting and high-quality fruits. A well-grown sapling should be 6-12 months old at the time of planting.

3. Spacing and Planting:

• Spacing: For high-density planting, maintain a spacing of 8m x 8m (156 trees per hectare). For traditional spacing, 10m x 10m (100 trees per hectare) can be used. • Planting Time: The best time for planting sapota is during the onset of the monsoon season (June–July), ensuring adequate rainfall for establishment. • Planting Method: Dig a hole slightly larger than the root ball of the sapling, ensuring the roots are spread out evenly and the sapling is planted at the same depth as it was in the nursery.

4. Irrigation:

• Watering Needs: Sapota requires regular irrigation, especially during the dry period. It is sensitive to water stress, so maintain adequate moisture levels. • Irrigation System: Drip irrigation is the most efficient method for sapota cultivation, providing water directly to the root zone without causing waterlogging. • Flood Irrigation: In areas where drip irrigation is not available, flooding the field with controlled water levels can be used. • Water Management: After flowering, reduce water levels slightly to avoid waterlogging, which can harm fruit development.

5. Fertilization:

• Initial Fertilization: Apply well-decomposed compost or farmyard manure (FYM) during planting. • Annual Fertilization: • Apply a balanced fertilizer (NPK) at planting time. • Top-dress with nitrogenous fertilizers like urea at 6 months and 12 months after planting. • For mature trees, use an NPK ratio of 10:10:10 or based on soil test recommendations. • Micronutrients: Sapota requires micronutrients like zinc, boron, and iron, which can be applied as foliar sprays if soil tests indicate deficiencies.

6. Weeding and Pest Control:

• Weeding: Regularly remove weeds manually or use herbicides during the early stages of growth. Maintain a clean field to avoid competition for nutrients and water. • Pest Control: Common pests include aphids, mealybugs, fruit flies, and scale insects. Neem oil or chemical pesticides can be used for pest management, depending on the severity of infestation. • Diseases: Sapota can be affected by fungal diseases like anthracnose and root rot. Use fungicides as needed, and maintain proper drainage to avoid waterlogging. .

7. Pruning:

• Pruning for Shape: Regularly prune the tree to maintain a good structure and remove dead or diseased branches. Proper pruning allows sunlight and air circulation, promoting better fruiting. • Annual Pruning: After harvesting, prune the trees to keep them manageable and healthy.

8. Harvesting:

• Harvesting Time: Sapota is ready for harvest when the fruits turn brownish and soften. The typical time from planting to fruit-bearing is about 4-5 years for grafted trees. • Method: Harvesting can be done manually by cutting the fruit from the tree with a sharp knife or scissors. Handle fruits carefully to avoid bruising. • Harvesting Indicators: The fruit should be soft, and the skin should turn brown or dark yellow. Avoid harvesting immature fruits, as they will not ripen properly.

9. Post-Harvest Handling:

• Cleaning: After harvesting, wash the fruits to remove dirt and debris. • Ripening: Allow fruits to ripen at room temperature. Once ripe, sapota should be consumed or sold immediately, as it has a short shelf life. • Storage: Sapota can be stored for short durations (2-3 days) in cool conditions. For long-term storage, refrigeration is required to prolong shelf life.

10.Expected Yield and Income:

• Yield per Tree (Mature Trees): 20-40 kg per year. • Yield per Acre (High-Density Planting): 10-12 tons per acre per year. • Yield per Acre (Traditional Spacing): 8-10 tons per acre per year. • Income per Acre: • High-density planting: ₹5,00,000 to ₹6,00,000 • Traditional spacing: ₹4,00,000 to ₹5,00,000 This makes sapota cultivation a highly profitable venture with substantial potential returns, especially as the trees mature and reach their full fruit-bearing potential.

11.Value-Added Products from Sapota:

1. Sapota Juice 2. Sapota Pulp 3. Dehydrated Sapota 4. Sapota Syrup 5. Sapota Ice Cream 6. Sapota Seed Oil 7. Sapota Cake (Animal Feed)
