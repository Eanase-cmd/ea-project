MANGO CULTIVATION METHOD

MANGO CULTIVATION METHOD:

Mango (Mangifera indica) is one of the most loved tropical fruits grown across the world. To ensure successful mango cultivation, proper planning, care, and maintenance are required. Here's a step-by-step guide for cultivating mangoes:

1. Site Selection and Land Preparation

• Climate: Mangoes thrive in tropical and subtropical climates. They require hot temperatures with temperatures between 24°C to 30°C for optimal growth.

• Soil Ph: soil pH should be of 5.5 to 7.5.

• Drainage: Ensure good drainage to prevent waterlogging, as mango trees are sensitive to water stagnation.

Land Preparation:

• Clear the land of weeds, rocks, and debris.

• Plough the soil to a depth of 25-30 cm.

• Dig planting pits of about 60-90 cm wide and 60-90 cm deep, spacing the pits 10-12 meters apart (ideal for commercial orchards).

• Fill the pits with a mixture of topsoil, compost, and well-rotted manure.

2. Choosing Mango Varieties

Popular Varieties:

• Alphonso: Known for its rich flavor, often considered the king of mangoes.

• Kesar: Smaller, sweet, and aromatic.

• Langra: A well-known variety in India with a unique taste

• Dasheri: Medium-sized, sweet, and juicy.

• Tommy Atkins: Popular in export markets due to its robustness

• Choose a variety that is suited to your local climate, soil conditions, and market demand.

3. Planting the Mango Tree

• Best Time to Plant: The ideal time to plant mango trees is during the onset of the rainy season (typically late spring to early summer) when the soil is moist and the tree has access to sufficient water.

Planting Depth:

• When planting, ensure the tree is placed at the same depth as it was in the nursery.

• Ensure the root ball is placed properly in the pit without disturbing the roots.

Spacing:

• 10-12 meters apart for large varieties (like Alphonso).

• 6-8 meters apart for dwarf varieties or small-scale planting.

Seed rate:

• 10-12 kg of mango seeds per hectare is typically used.

• This is for direct seed sowing in a nursery bed or field

4. Watering

• Young Trees: Water the newly planted mango tree regularly, especially in dry periods, ensuring the soil is kept moist but not waterlogged.

• Mature Trees: Mango trees are drought-tolerant but need consistent watering during flowering and fruit-setting. Use drip irrigation to provide water directly to the roots and minimize evaporation.

5. Fertilization

Mango trees need essential nutrients like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium for healthy growth and fruiting.

• Apply well-rotted manure or compost annually around the base of the tree.

Fertilize with NPK fertilizers in the growing season:

• Nitrogen for growth.

• Phosphorus for root and flower development.

• Potassium for fruiting and resistance to disease

• Micronutrients like zinc and boron can be applied to enhance flowering and fruiting.

6. Pruning and Shaping

Pruning Young Trees:

• Remove any dead, diseased, or damaged branches.

• Trim the tree to maintain an open center, which improves air circulation and light penetration.

• Prune to a single central stem in the first few years to encourage better branching.

Pruning Mature Trees:

• After the harvest, prune the tree to remove old branches and encourage new growth.

• Mango trees need moderate pruning to help shape the tree and improve fruiting.

7. Pest and Disease Control

Pests:

• Common pests include mealybugs, aphids, fruit flies, and caterpillars.

• Use organic pesticides or neem oil to control these pests.

Diseases:

• Anthracnose (fungal disease) is a common problem and can cause fruit rot.

• Powdery mildew and bacterial canker are also common issues.

• Apply fungicides to control fungal diseases and ensure proper air circulation around the tree to prevent infection.

8. Flowering and Fruit Setting

• Mango trees typically begin to flower in the winter months (December to January).

Flowering: Mango trees produce small, fragrant flowers that form in large panicles.

• These flowers are self-pollinating, but cross-pollination by bees or wind can enhance fruit yield.

Fruit Setting: Mangoes take 3-5 months to mature after flowering, depending on the variety.

• Ensure proper watering and fertilization during this time to support fruit development.

9. Harvesting

Maturity Indicators:

• Mangoes are ready to harvest when they have reached their full size and color. • Depending on the variety, mangoes will start to turn color (yellow, red, or green) as they ripen. • Gently press the fruit to check if it is slightly soft, indicating ripeness.

Harvesting Method:

• Use sharp pruning shears to cut the fruit off the tree, leaving a small portion of the stem attached to the fruit. • Handle the fruit gently to avoid bruising.

• Post-Harvest: Mangoes should be stored in a cool, dry place. For export purposes, they may be packaged and stored in cold storage to preserve freshness.

10. Expected Yield and Income

Young Trees: For the first few years, the yield is low.

Mature Trees:

• 10-15 tons per hectare in the 7th to 10th year for large commercial varieties. • 25 tons per hectare for high-yield varieties (like Alphonso) under optimal conditions.

Income:

Market Price: ₹30 to ₹150 per kg, depending on the variety and quality.

Example Calculation:

• 10 tons at ₹50 per kg = ₹500,000. • 15 tons at ₹50 per kg = ₹750,000. • 25 tons at ₹100 per kg (premium varieties like Alphonso) = ₹2,500,000.

Production Costs:

• Total Costs (labor, irrigation, fertilizers, etc.): ₹100,000 to ₹150,000 per hectare annually.

Net Profit:

• After deducting production costs, farmers can expect a net profit of ₹350,000 to ₹600,000 per hectare for regular varieties and ₹2,000,000 or more per hectare for premium varieties.
