CASHEW CULTIVATION METHOD

CASHEW CULTIVATION METHOD:

Cashew is a tropical tree nut crop that grows well in warm, humid environments. It is primarily grown for its seeds, which are used in food products, and its kernels are highly sought after in global markets. Below is a detailed guide to cultivating cashew, including land preparation, seed rate, soil requirements, expected yield, income, and value-added products.

1. Land Preparation

• Field Selection: Cashew grows best in well-drained sandy loam or red loamy soils. It prefers slightly acidic to neutral soils with a pH between 5.5 to 7.0. Cashew trees thrive in regions with moderate rainfall (1000-2000 mm annually).

• Land Clearing: Clear the field of weeds, stumps, and debris. The land should be cleared and levelled properly to prevent waterlogging.

• Ploughing and Tilling: The field should be ploughed thoroughly to a depth of 30-35 cm to break up the soil and improve aeration. This helps in root development and water drainage.

• Fertilization: Apply well-rotted organic manure (FYM or compost) before planting to improve soil fertility. Cashew trees generally require phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium but can fix nitrogen in the soil.

• Recommended Fertilizer Application: For young cashew plants, apply 50 kg of farmyard manure (FYM) per plant during planting. After 2-3 years, apply balanced fertilizers like NPK (20:20:20) in split doses.

2. Seed Selection and Planting

Seed Quality: Use high-quality seeds or grafted seedlings for better yields and disease resistance. Grafted cashew plants generally perform better than seed-grown ones, producing better quality nuts and yielding quicker.

Varieties: Some popular cashew varieties include:

• Vengurla-4

• Bhaskara

• Goa-1

• Dhana (for drought-prone areas)

Seed Rate: If growing from seeds, 8-10 kg of seeds are needed per hectare. However, if using grafted seedlings, you may need around 300 to 400 plants per hectare.

Spacing: Proper spacing is crucial for optimal tree growth. The recommended spacing is:

• Tree-to-tree: 7-8 meters

• Row-to-row: 7-8 meters

Planting Depth: Dig planting holes of approximately 30 cm x 30 cm x 30 cm and fill them with a mixture of soil and compost. Plant the seedlings in these holes at the same depth they were previously growing.

3. Irrigation

Water Requirements: Cashew trees are drought-resistant once established, but they need water during the flowering and nut development stages for optimal yield.

Irrigation in Initial Stages: During the first year, ensure the plants receive regular water to promote growth. Drip irrigation is recommended, as it ensures efficient water usage and helps prevent waterlogging.

Post-Planting Irrigation: Once the trees are well-established (2-3 years), they become drought-tolerant, but during dry spells, additional watering will help maintain yields.

4. Weeding and Intercultural Operations

Weeding: Cashew trees need to be protected from weeds, especially during the early growth stages. Weeding should be done manually or using herbicides as required. Weeding is particularly important in the first 2-3 years of establishment.

Mulching: Mulch the base of the trees with organic material (e.g., leaves, grass, or straw) to retain soil moisture, improve soil fertility, and prevent weed growth.

Pruning: Regularly prune dead, diseased, or overcrowded branches to allow better air circulation and sunlight penetration. Also, prune the trees to ensure that the canopy does not become too dense, as this can hinder the growth of new flowers and fruit.

5. Pest and Disease Management

Common Pests:

• Tea mosquito bug: Causes damage to tender shoots, leading to wilting.

• Root mealy bug: Affects root health.

• Caterpillars and borers: Damage flowers and nuts.

• Termites: Affect the roots and stems.

Common Diseases:

• Anthracnose (fungal infection affecting leaves and fruits).

• Powdery mildew.

• Cashew rust (affects leaves).

• Crown rot (fungal infection that affects the base of the trunk).

Control Measures: Use organic or chemical pesticides as necessary to control pests and diseases. Prune infected plant parts, and ensure that you are not over-watering the plants as this can encourage disease.

6. Flowering and Pollination

Flowering: Cashew trees typically begin to flower in the second to third year after planting. The flowering period is usually during the dry season.

Pollination: Cashew flowers are self-pollinating but may benefit from the presence of bees for better pollination. While they are tolerant of moderate wind, bee activity boosts the number of pollinated flowers and fruit set.

7.Harvesting

Time to Harvest: Cashew trees take about 3-4 years to begin bearing fruits (nuts) and 7-8 years to achieve full maturity. The fruits mature in about 120-150 days after flowering.

Signs of Maturity: The cashew apples turn yellow or red when mature. The cashew nuts at the base of the apples will start to loosen and fall off the tree when they are ready to be harvested.

Harvesting Method: Cashews are usually hand-harvested. The nuts should be collected as soon as they fall to prevent them from being damaged by the ground. The harvested nuts are then separated from the fruit (cashew apple).

8.Post-Harvest Handling

Drying: After harvesting, the cashew nuts should be sun-dried for 1-2 days to reduce moisture content to around 10-12%. This prevents mode formation during storage.

Shelling: The dried cashew nuts are then shell cracked to remove the hard shell and obtain the nut kernel.

Storage: After shelling, store the cashew kernels in airtight containers in a cool, dry place to avoid spoilage and contamination.

Cashew Apple Processing: The cashew apple is also a valuable byproduct and can be processed into juices, jams, or even alcoholic beverages.

9. Yield and Expected Income

Expected Yield:

• Average Yield: A mature cashew tree can yield approximately 5-8 kg of nuts per year. In optimal conditions, yields can be 12-15 kg per tree.

• Per Hectare Yield: For a plantation with 300-400 trees per hectare, the expected yield is 1,500-3,000 kg of cashew nuts per hectare annually.

Income:

• Price of Cashew Nuts: ₹600-₹800 per kg (prices can vary based on market conditions and quality).

• Total Income: For a yield of 1,500 kg at ₹700 per kg, the income would be approximately ₹1,05,000 per hectare.

• For a higher yield of 3,000 kg at ₹700 per kg, the income could be around ₹2,10,000 per hectare.

10. Value-Added Products

• Cashew Kernel

• Cashew Butter

• Cashew Oil

• Cashew Apple Juice

• Cashew Shell Liquid (CSL)

Cashew cultivation is a long-term investment, but it can be highly profitable once the trees mature and produce high-quality nuts. Proper management of irrigation, pest control, and post-harvest handling is crucial for maximizing yields and ensuring the profitability of the crop. By producing both cashew nuts and value-added products like juice and oil, farmers can diversify their income and tap into multiple markets.
