PIGEON PEA CULTIVATION METHOD

PIGEON PEA CULTIVATION METHOD

pea, also known as toor dal, arhar, or red gram, is a leguminous crop widely grown for its protein-rich seeds. It is a hardy, drought-resistant plant that thrives well in tropical and subtropical climates. Below is a comprehensive guide on how to cultivate pigeon pea effectively, including land preparation, seed rate, soil conditions, yield expectations, income potential, and value-added products

1. Land Preparation

Field Selection: Pigeon pea grows well in well-drained loamy or sandy loam soils with good organic matter content. It prefers a neutral to slightly alkaline soil with a pH range of 6.0-7.5

Ploughing and Tilling: The land should be thoroughly ploughed and tilled to a depth of 20-25 cm to break up the soil and facilitate root development. If the soil is hard or compact, deep tilling may be required.

Levelling: Level the field after ploughing to ensure uniform distribution of water during irrigation and prevent waterlogging

Fertilization: Prior to planting, incorporate well-rotted compost or farmyard manure into the soil to enhance fertility. Pigeon pea is a legume, so it can fix its own nitrogen; therefore, it requires low nitrogen fertilizer but benefits from phosphorus and potassium.

• Recommended fertilizer application: 20-30 kg of Nitrogen, 40 kg of Phosphorus, and 20 kg of Potassium per hectare.

2. Seed Selection

Seed Quality: Use high-quality, disease-free seeds from a certified source. Pigeon pea has self-pollinating flowers, and selecting good seeds ensures better yields.

Varieties: There are both early maturing and long-duration varieties of pigeon pea. Common varieties include:

• Early maturing varieties: UPAS-120, ICPL-88039

• Medium and long-duration varieties: Asha, BDN-2, PKV-T-2

Seed Rate: The seed rate typically ranges from 10 to 12 kg per hectare depending on the sowing method and spacing

3.Sowing

Ideal Season: Pigeon pea is a rainfed crop, typically sown at the beginning of the rainy season (May-June). The ideal time for sowing depends on the regional monsoon season.

Spacing: Pigeon pea requires adequate spacing to grow well. The recommended spacing is:

• Row-to-row: 45-75 cm

• Plant-to-plant: 15-30 cm

Sowing Depth: Seeds should be sown at a depth of 3-5 cm in moist, well-prepared soil. Seedlings can also be transplanted, but direct sowing is preferred.

4.Irrigation

Water Requirements: Pigeon pea is drought-tolerant but needs adequate moisture during the flowering and pod formation stages for better yields.

First Irrigation: Provide light irrigation immediately after sowing to ensure seed germination

Subsequent Irrigation: Irrigation should be provided when the crop shows signs of water stress, particularly during the flowering and pod development stages. Avoid over-watering, as it can lead to root rot.

Irrigation Method: Drip or furrow irrigation is ideal for water management. Ensure that water does not stagnate, as this can damage the crop.

5. Weeding and Intercultural Operations

Weeding: Pigeon pea requires regular weeding to reduce competition for nutrients, water, and light. Weeding should be done 2-3 times during the growing season.

Mulching: Apply organic mulch (e.g., straw, leaves, or grass) to help conserve soil moisture, suppress weeds, and improve soil structure.

Earthing Up: After 30-45 days of sowing, earthing up or mound soil around the base of the plant helps protect the roots and improves plant stability.

Supporting Plants: As pigeon pea plants grow, they may need support. If grown in areas with wind, staking or using trellises might be necessary to prevent them from falling over.

6. Pest and Disease Management

Pests: Common pests in pigeon pea cultivation include:

• Pod borers (Helicoverpa armigera)

• Aphids

• Whiteflies

• Root-knot nematodes

Disease Management: Pigeon pea is prone to several diseases, including:

• Wilt (Fusarium oxysporum)

• Powdery mildew

• Phytophthora blight

• Leaf spot disease

Control Measures: Use appropriate pesticides or bio-pesticides to manage pests. Prevent disease outbreaks by practicing crop rotation, using disease-resistant varieties, and applying fungicides if necessary.

7. Harvesting

Time to Harvest: Pigeon pea takes approximately 160-200 days to mature, depending on the variety and climatic conditions.

Signs of Maturity: Harvest when the pods are fully mature, and the leaves start turning yellow or drying up. The pods should have a firm texture, and the seeds inside should rattle when shaken.

Harvesting Method: Harvest the crop by pulling out the plants or cutting them with a sickle or harvesting knife. After harvesting, the plants should be left to dry in the field for a few days.

Manual Harvesting: For manual harvesting, cut the plants carefully to avoid damaging the pods.

8.Post-Harvest Handling

Threshing: After drying the plants, threshing is done to separate the seeds from the pods. This can be done manually or using mechanical threshers.

Cleaning: Clean the seeds to remove any dirt or debris. You can use a sieve or air blower to clean the seeds thoroughly.

Storage: Store the cleaned seeds in cool, dry places in ventilated containers to prevent spoilage. Keep the seeds away from moisture and insects.

Processing: Pigeon peas can also be processed into split peas, flour, or used for making dal.

9. Yield

Expected Yield: The average yield of pigeon pea ranges from 1,000 to 1,500 kg per hectare depending on soil fertility, irrigation, pest control, and variety used. In some cases, yields can go higher, especially with high-yielding varieties and good management practices.

Harvesting Period: The crop typically takes 5-6 months to mature.

10. Expected Yield and Income

Market Price of Pigeon Pea: ₹60-₹80 per kg (prices can vary based on region and market demand)

Income Calculation:

• For 1,000 kg at ₹70 per kg: ₹70,000

• For 1,500 kg at ₹70 per kg: ₹1,05,000

Potential Profit (per hectare):

• Net Income: ₹50,000 - ₹1,00,000 (depending on yield and market conditions).

• Break-even Point: To break even, if the yield is low (around 1,000 kg), a farmer should aim for a price of ₹60-₹70 per kg to cover cultivation costs.

11. Value-Added Products

• Pigeon Pea Dal

• Pigeon Pea Flour

• Pigeon Pea Oil

• Animal Feed

• Seed Processing

Pigeon pea is a versatile and economically significant legume crop. It requires careful attention to soil preparation, pest management, irrigation, and harvesting for optimal yields. The crop offers an excellent opportunity for smallholder farmers, with multiple value-added products to increase profitability. With proper management, pigeon pea can be a high-yielding crop that contributes significantly to farm income.
